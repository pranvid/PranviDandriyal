01

The problem

Interviews are the highest-stakes, least-structured step in hiring.

Decades of research show structured interviews predict job performance far better than unstructured ones — yet in practice, most interviews are still improvised. Interviewers rely on memory, take rushed notes, and form opinions before a candidate has finished speaking. The result is inconsistent evaluations, bias that's hard to see, and candidates who can't tell whether they got a fair shot.

SHL saw an opportunity: a live video-interview product that builds structure into the moment of the interview itself — and captures the competency data that structure produces. The design challenge was to add rigor without making interviewers feel policed or slowing them down.

03

What I learned from interviewers

I started with a survey of 50 experienced interviewers in the US and UK (recruited through Prolific, all with 6+ months of hiring responsibility), then ran usability sessions to watch how people actually run interviews. Three findings shaped everything that followed.

1. Notes are the bottleneck

Most interviewers took notes on paper, while the candidate was still speaking — splitting their attention at the exact moment they should be listening. When I mapped time allocation, note-taking was where they spent the most time and wanted to spend the least. What they wanted more of was discussing the candidate with their team afterward.

2. Guides exist, but everyone branches out

Interviewers had guides — usually a Word doc of questions — but rarely asked them verbatim. They skipped questions, improvised follow-ups, and used the guide as an answer sheet for structure. A rigid script would have been rejected; the design had to support structure and deviation.

3. Judgment forms early, so scoring has to be present

Many interviewers formed an opinion while the candidate was still speaking. That's precisely where bias creeps in. Scoring couldn't be a form you fill out afterward from memory — it had to be visible during the conversation, anchored to criteria.

The insight

Interviewers didn't need to be told to be structured. They needed the structure to be available in the moment — the guide, the scoring criteria, and their notes in one place, without looking away from the candidate.

04

The design

I designed the interview as three connected moments — prepare, interview, evaluate — with the guide, scoring rubric, and notes always within reach.

Prepare without leaving the tool

Interviewers open into context: the candidate's résumé and assessment reports, the job description, the evaluation topics, and the scoring guide — so they walk in knowing the role, the person, and the rating scale.

IMG · prep / onboarding screen

Guide and score, side by side

During the interview, questions stay accessible so interviewers can plan follow-ups on the fly. Scientifically curated rubrics sit next to each competency, so rating happens against defined criteria — not a gut feeling. A big video keeps rapport human; notes live right beside the question they belong to, because research showed notes and questions are inseparable.

IMG · live interview — guide, rubric, notes

A scorecard that shows its work

Evaluation pulls candidate responses, observations, competency scores, and the notes that justify each rating into one review — with an editable, transcript-and-notes-based summary. Every recommendation is backed by evidence a hiring manager can trace, not a single number.

IMG · interview scorecard

Playback for shared decisions

Recording plus scorecard lets multiple stakeholders review the same interview later — so decisions get made on evidence, together, instead of relying on one interviewer's memory.

05

Analytics: making fairness measurable

The structured data the interview produces becomes an analytics layer for talent teams. It surfaces how interviews actually run — preparation rates, punctuality, whether scoring guides were referenced, time spent per phase, candidate NPS — so organizations can spot best practices, catch subconscious bias, and train interviewers at scale.

Designing this meant making a genuinely dense dashboard legible: dozens of metrics, benchmarked against company averages, without burying the signal. This was the piece I'm proudest of — it's where good interviewing becomes something you can manage, not just hope for.

07

Reflection

This project taught me to design for two audiences at once: the interviewer who has to use the tool under pressure, and the business that needs the tool to pay off. The winning move was small — putting scoring and notes where the interviewer is already looking — but it only worked because the research told me exactly where their attention already was.

The work was recognized company-wide for its business impact. The natural next step, which I began scoping, was turning the analytics into customer-facing reports that help clients improve their own interview practices over time.
